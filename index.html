Risk Management & Compliance Analyst II
State of South Carolina Columbia, SC May 2024 — Present
● Active

This is the role where everything I'd learned started coming together. I went from monitoring alerts to actually shaping how an entire state agency thinks about risk — and that shift changed how I approach every problem.

Day-to-day is vulnerability management across a large, complex infrastructure — scanning with Nessus and Microsoft Defender, triaging findings by real business impact, and making sure remediation actually happens rather than just getting tracked in a spreadsheet. I align everything to NIST 800-53, but more importantly, I try to make the controls meaningful rather than checkbox exercises.

"I once traced an alert back to a misconfigured conditional access policy that had been sitting quietly for months. That kind of thing keeps you humble — and keeps you thorough."

One project I'm most proud of: a Python automation pipeline that ingests MS-ISAC threat intel and automatically pushes malicious IPs/domains into Cisco Umbrella via API. Threat gets flagged, blocks go up — sometimes before our tools have even processed the initial alert.

Datics was a smaller shop, which meant wearing more hats and making calls without always having a senior analyst to check with. That independence pushed me forward faster than any structured program would have.

Most of my work split between Splunk and QRadar — monitoring, triaging, and investigating alerts across client environments. Medium-severity incidents I owned completely. For high-stakes ones, I assembled a full picture before escalating so the response team wasn't starting from zero.

Working at Dell meant operating at scale. The infrastructure was massive, alert volume was high, and the stakes for getting things wrong were real. It was the environment that taught me discipline and process.

High Radius Bangalore, India Jun 2019 — May 2020
Where it started

I'll be honest — I had no idea what I was doing when I started here. Theory from class felt completely different from a live Splunk dashboard with real alerts coming in during actual shifts.

Senior analysts were patient. They showed me how to read logs, how to ask the right questions before jumping to conclusions, and how to write up findings clearly enough that someone else could pick up where you left off. Those habits stuck with me through everything that followed.

"My first useful contribution was a PowerShell script that flagged unusual login times. Small thing — but it caught something real, and that feeling never got old."

I wrote documentation, helped triage alerts, and slowly got comfortable with the rhythm of SOC work — the shift handoffs, the false positive rate, the occasional thing that turns out to be genuinely alarming. It was unglamorous and I loved it.
